☀ The Powerhouse: ESM Generation

The European Service Module serves as the primary power plant for Orion. It features four distinct solar array wings, spanning roughly 19 meters across. These arrays consist of approximately 15,000 highly efficient gallium arsenide cells. This system continuously captures solar energy during transit, converting it to electrical power to run critical systems and recharge the internal batteries.

Power is distributed across various subsystems based on critical mission needs. Life support and avionics require the most stable and significant power draws during nominal flight operations.

🔋 Energy Storage: Crew Module Batteries

While the ESM generates primary power, the Crew Module (CM) houses four 120-volt Lithium-ion batteries. These are critical during specific mission phases where solar generation is impossible or insufficient, such as orbital eclipses, launch operations, and most importantly, the final re-entry phase.

Prior to re-entering Earth's atmosphere, the ESM is jettisoned. From that moment until splashdown and recovery, the CM relies entirely on these four batteries. The system is designed with strict redundancy to ensure life support and flight computers remain active even if one battery fails during descent.

Mission Power Demand Timeline

The roughly 10-day Artemis II lunar flyby mission presents dynamic electrical loads. Power demand peaks during major engine burns like the Trans-Lunar Injection (TLI) and orbital adjustments due to active thermal and propulsion system requirements. During the translunar coast, the spacecraft enters a stable, lower-power configuration.
